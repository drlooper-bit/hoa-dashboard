Case Overview

Allegation

The TownCrier ("Doesn't add UP!") investigation asserts that Plantation at Leesburg HOA governance and accounting contain material irregularities involving:

  • An authorized $42,300/month draw to support golf operations that may have been misclassified, partially omitted from the Balance Sheet, and accompanied by an alleged January 2025 excess draw of ~$50,000.
  • A fabricated December 2025 year-end financial package, including a reported $4,876,898.14 Total Reserve Funding line that appears implausible relative to monthly norms.
  • A December 2025 year-end balance sheet that does not balance (Total Assets $1,838,775.84 vs Total Liabilities & Equity $5,122,035.42).
  • A phantom $453,129.67 profit posted in December 2025 and deleted in January 2026.
  • An effective collapse of HOA equity from ~$7,767,960 (FY2024 audited) to $48,692 (April 2026).
  • Claims that SPA 2021 revenue vanishes from January–March 2026 statements despite continued collections.

The December 2025 balance sheet shows a distinct Total Net Income / (Loss) … $ 453,129.67 line. The January 2026 balance sheet shows this line at $207.58 then $50.00. The entry disappears entirely between year-end and January 1, 2026 — a literal match to the alleged sequence.

F3 — December 2025 year-end financials are mechanically implausible

December 2025 consolidated income statement reports Total Revenue of only $37,746.30 against a ~$450K–$507K monthly run-rate. Total Reserve Funding shows a single $4,876,898.14 line with no movement pattern. Utilities appear only in the total column with no Operating/SA breakdown. These are not credible outputs from a functioning accounting system.

F4 — December 2025 balance sheet does not balance

From the December 2025 balance sheet: Total Assets = $1,838,775.84; Total Liabilities & Equity = $5,122,035.42. A $3.28M difference that violates the fundamental accounting equation.

F5 — Effective equity collapse 2024 to 2026

FY2024 audited balance sheet: Total Equity = $7,767,959.65. April 2026 consolidated balance sheet: Total Equity = $48,691.69. Equity depletion of approximately $7.72M over roughly two years.

F6 — Widespread monthly statement integrity failures

January 2026 consolidated income statement: Operating $9,879.81 + Special Assessment $17,201.68 ≠ Total $933.05 — arithmetic impossible. Multiple months show single-digit or triple-dollar operating results against ~$500K revenue run-rates. These are characteristic of retroactive modification or templating errors, not normal period-close output.

Governing Statute / Compliance

Statutory Framework — Florida Homeowners' Association Act (Ch. 720, Fla. Stat.) and Related Laws

The TownCrier findings are assessed against the following regulatory baseline:

  • Chapter 720, Florida Statutes (Homeowners' Association Act) — Primary authority governing daily operations, board duties, and dispute resolution. Key provisions include:
    • § 720.303(4) & (8) — Official records access and mandatory website posting for associations with 100+ parcels effective Jan. 1, 2025. Governing rules, active contracts, budgets, insurance policies, and minutes must be downloadable by members.
    • § 720.303(6) & (7) — Annual budget preparation and audit requirement. HOAs with annual revenues ≥ $500,000 must have an annual audit by a certified public accountant.
    • § 720.303(2) — Meetings must be open to members; closed meetings allowed only for attorney-client privileged matters and personnel issues.
    • § 720.303(3) — 48-hour conspicuous physical posting requirement for meeting notice.
    • § 720.3033 — Fiduciary duties of directors/officers; mandatory board education (4-hour curriculum within 90 days of taking office, renewed every four years; annual continuing education requirements). Failure to file certificate results in automatic suspension from the board.
    • § 720.3035 — Limits on architectural control covenants.
    • § 720.304 / 720.3045 — Protects member property rights, including artificial turf, vegetable gardens, clotheslines, holiday decorations, trash cans, and first-responder vehicle parking.
    • § 720.305 — Rigorous due process for fines and suspensions: 14-day written notice of right to hearing; independent committee of non-board neighbors; 90-day hearing window; cure provision bars penalty if violation is corrected before hearing.
    • § 720.3085 — Assessment collection process, late fees, lien creation, and simple-interest-only rule (compound interest banned even if governing documents permit it).
    • § 720.309 — Special assessments for capital improvements require 14-day advance notice and approval by majority or supermajority of voting interests. Special assessment procedure is actively litigated in Fredericks/Eisenhower v. Plantation at Leesburg HOA.
    • § 720.311 — Mandatory pre-suit mediation before filing circuit-court actions regarding covenant enforcement, assessments, or official records access.
    • § 720.303(4) & (5) — Record retention for 7 years; member inspection/copying rights within 10 business days of written request; minimum damages of $50/day for up to 10 days for non-compliance.
  • Chapter 617, Florida Statutes (Not-for-Profit Corporations Act) — Because HOAs are incorporated as nonprofit corporations, corporate governance, voting, and dissolution rules apply.
  • Chapter 468, Florida Statutes, Part VIII (Community Association Managers) — Licensed property managers must disclose conflicts of interest, handle service transitions within 20 business days of contract termination, and complete HOA-specific continuing education.
  • HB 1203 and companion reforms — Recent legislative overhaul significantly tightened HOA regulation, including digital-portal mandates, debit-card prohibitions, tiered financial reporting thresholds, and board-education requirements.
  • FS 812.014 (theft) — Unauthorized use of association-issued debit cards by board members, employees, or managers constitutes criminal theft.

Application to Plantation at Leesburg

Based on extracted records and case filings, the following statutory compliance issues are apparent:

  • Website/posted documents — § 720.303(4) & (8): Residents allege 2026 bids/contracts missing from the HOA website; board acknowledges update is "ongoing." If required documents are not posted by the statutory deadline, this is a direct violation.
  • Audits suspended — § 720.303(7): 2023 and 2024 audits are incomplete because auditors withdrew; board expects an unqualified opinion but has no firm completion date. With ~2,820 homes, HOA revenue almost certainly exceeds the $500,000 audit threshold.
  • Record inspection delays — § 720.303(4) & (5): Residents allege selective or delayed access; financial postings appear incomplete. Non-compliance triggers statutory minimum damages.
  • Reserve accounting / equity transfers: Opaque transfers between discretionary reserves and equity accounts raise questions under fiduciary-duty standards and reserve-funding rules.
  • Golf course draw — § 720.3085: $42,300/month authorized draw to golf operations, potentially misclassified or partially omitted from balance sheet. The monthly statements show contra-revenue lines whose treatment requires management and audit review.
  • Common-area maintenance — § 720.303: Cranes Roost irrigation failure for 2+ months with no repair and continuing revenue loss supports a breach-of-fiduciary-duty or failure-to-maintain claim.
  • Special assessment — § 720.309: Challenged in Fredericks/Eisenhower v. Plantation at Leesburg HOA; board claims notice/vote compliance, plaintiffs allege impropriety. Not yet resolved.
  • Debit card prohibition — FS 812.014: Board members, employees, or managers using association debit cards for unauthorized expenses commit criminal theft. Any such use must be investigated.
  • Board education — § 720.3033: Directors must complete 4-hour curriculum within 90 days of taking office, renewed every four years; annual continuing education required. Failure suspends board eligibility. Directors' training certificates should be verified.
  • Digital portal — HB 1203 / § 720.303(4): Mandatory secure portal for 100+ parcel associations; requires downloadable governing rules, active contracts, budgets, insurance, and minutes.

Verdict on Statutory Compliance

There are multiple strong prima facie claims of Florida HOA statute violations: missing website/posted contracts (§ 720.303(4) and (8)); incomplete audits (§ 720.303(7)); record-inspection delays (§ 720.303(5)); reserve-account opacity; maintenance failures supporting fiduciary-breach claims; and active special-assessment litigation (§ 720.309). The financial irregularities documented in this dashboard compound these compliance concerns and support a forensic accounting review under Florida HOA governance standards.

Legal Analysis

Corroborated but Not Fully Proved

The ledgers corroborate the TownCrier's structural allegations: the HOA's December 2025 year-end package contains a non-balancing balance sheet, and clearly fabricated monthly totals. The blog's framing of "doesn't add up" is materially correct at the aggregate level.

Unauthorized Draw Hypothesis — Partially Corroborated

The $42,300/month authorized draw is confirmed as real and consistent. The blog's claim that this amount is misclassified or partially omitted is not falsifiable from the current extraction; the monthly statements show a contra-revenue line whose treatment requires management and audit review. The alleged January 2025 ~$50,000 excess draw remains plausible but unproven from the current set, pending a month-level cash-flow or due-to/from reconciliation for 2023–2025.

Equity Destruction Vector

The documented move from ~$7.77M equity to ~$48.7K equity in two years is an extreme out-of-ordinary result. The December 2025 fabrication and the dangling $3.28M balance-sheet gap are concrete numeric evidence of accounting irregularities, not normal variance.

Burden Management and Internal Controls

A December 2025 balance sheet that does not balance by $3.28M, a phantom profit posted and then reversed, and January 2026 monthly arithmetic errors all point to material internal control failures. Under Florida HOA governance expectations, these findings support a forensic accounting review and, depending on membership composition and charter provisions, potential breach-of-fiduciary-duty analysis.
